Like I said, we're using Ogg, and it sounds awesome no matter how low you
crank the bitrate. I'm trying to find the sweetspot right now, but Oggs are
notorious for sounding clear.

On 1/20/07, Marek M. <wansti at gmx.de> wrote:
>
> Actually, I found it pretty easy to get MIDI working. Getting a software
> synthesizer to run was a bit tricky, but no black magic. The problem
> with shipping MIDI is that the music will sound totally different on
> every machine. The music files I'm currently producing are not in the
> General MIDI range, so I'd have to rewrite almost every song. Also, I'm
> rendering them on a thousand-dollar tone generator - I sincerely hope
> that there is no way of getting the same quality for free. :-)
> Anyway, I think that from an artist's point of view, not having the
> slightest idea of what your work will sound like on the players'
> computers is not acceptable, especially when it's just for saving a few
> megabytes. I remember playing old games that came with MIDI files
> because they had to fit on a few floppy disks (for example, old
> LucasArts adventures). I used to complain about how crappy the music was
> because I had a crappy sound card. Rendering the same MIDI files on
> modern hardware makes me feel sympathy for the composers, because only a
> small percentage of the audience really got an idea of what the songs
> were intended to sound like: Namely, other musicians who used the same
> hardware. I discovered that tunes that sounded like cheap background
> music on a SoundBlaster16 were actually works of high composing skill. I
> still have a long way to go before I can write music like they can, but
> I think we should make use of the ways we have now to make the music
> sound as good as possible. I agree that the tarball is pretty big, but
> file size isn't that much of a problem anymore. Especially people who
> play games are used to much bigger downloads.
>
> As for reducing the quality: I currently compress the songs to 160kb
> Ogg, and even with that relatively high quality rate, the difference to
> the uncompressed originals is clearly audible (which really surprised
> me). Especially in the high-frequency range, such as the piano part on
> bossattack.ogg. I'll still have to try using stronger compression but
> I'm sure I won't like the results. Maybe that's just because I've heard
> them uncompressed, and because I know every single note of every song -
> so maybe I'm not the one who should judge over whether the quality is
> sufficient. I'll also try fiddling with my recording chain to squeeze a
> little more quality out of it at the same compression rate. And I guess
> I could go down to 128kb if size really matters to you. :-) But I
> wouldn't like to go below that.
>
> As for the sound track CD that is supposed to be released with Milestone
> 2, I'm planning to make it available as a disc image containing the
> uncompressed music. I wonder how that will affect the decision on the
> in-game quality. Will people notice the difference? And if they do, will
> they care? And if they care, will they bother to compress the tracks
> themselves and replace the ones we shipped? Or will they want to know
> why we didn't do so ourselves?
>
> Sorry for the long rant. But I think that the loss of quality that comes
> with stronger compression doesn't justify the few megabytes we'd save.
>
>     Marek
